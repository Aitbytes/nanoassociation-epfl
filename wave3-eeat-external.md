## 7. E-E-A-T and External Authority Signals

### 7.1 The Definitive Answer: Framework, Not Ranking Factor

**E-E-A-T is NOT a direct ranking factor — it is a quality evaluation framework.** This is confirmed directly by Google's own documentation and every authoritative source consulted.

Google's Danny Sullivan (2019): _"Our automated systems use a mix of many different signals to rank great content. We've tried to make this mix align what human beings would agree is great content as they would assess it according to E-A-T criteria."_

Google's Gary Illyes: _"E-A-T is largely based on links and mentions on authoritative sites."_ — confirming it works through proxy signals, not direct scoring.

Google's Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines (public document): E-E-A-T is the rubric used by human Quality Raters — their feedback trains Google's algorithms but they have no direct control over rankings.

**How E-E-A-T actually works:**

```
E-E-A-T Framework
       ↓
Google trains algorithms to identify E-E-A-T qualities
       ↓
Algorithms use proxy signals: links, mentions, brand searches,
content characteristics, user engagement
       ↓
Quality Raters evaluate results using E-E-A-T criteria
       ↓
Feedback loop improves algorithms
       ↓
Content exhibiting E-E-A-T qualities ranks better
```

The March 2024 Core Update folded the Helpful Content System into core ranking — making E-E-A-T-aligned content more influential than ever.

**Kyle Roof's practical framing:** _"E-E-A-T is not going to move your rank directly. It's about maintaining your rank once Google does come and do that check. You keep ranked if established. Another site that got tagged because they lacked it? You go up when they drop."_

## 7.2 The Four E-E-A-T Components in Depth

### Experience (E) — Added December 2022

Experience reflects **firsthand, real-world engagement** with a topic.

**Google's definition (Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines):** Content demonstrates first-hand expertise when the creator has actually used a product, visited a place, or performed the task they're describing.

**How Experience is demonstrated by content type:**

| Content Type         | Experience Signals                                                                                      | Source                |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------- |
| **Product reviews**  | Personal photos/videos of product, actual test results, own purchase receipts, weeks of use documented  | Semrush, David Gunner |
| **Travel guides**    | Personal photos from visits, GPS-tagged images, date-stamped, specific recommendations from being there | Google's QRG          |
| **How-to tutorials** | Screenshots from actual implementation, personal results shown, failures/successes documented           | Matthew Zare          |
| **News**             | Being at the scene, primary sourcing, eyewitness accounts                                               | Google's QRG          |
| **Food/cooking**     | Videos of cooking, photos of finished dishes, personal modifications                                    | Google's QRG          |
| **Research blogs**   | Original data, experiments conducted personally, "I ran this test"                                      | David Gunner          |

**Key finding (Ahrefs study cited by David Gunner):** _"86.5% of content ranking in the top 20 search results shows first-hand experience or expert insight. Content that's just theory is getting left in the dust."_

**Source: Google Search Central (Dec 2025 official documentation):** _"Does your content clearly demonstrate first-hand expertise and a depth of knowledge (for example, expertise that comes from having actually used a product or service, or visiting a place)?"_

---

### Expertise (E)

Expertise reflects **demonstrated knowledge** — either formal credentials or deep acquired knowledge. It differs from Experience: Experience is _firsthand participation_; Expertise is _demonstrated knowledge_.

**Formal Expertise vs. Experiential Expertise:**

| Type                       | Required For                                         | How to Demonstrate                                               |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Formal expertise**       | YMYL topics (health, finance, legal, medical)        | Degrees, certifications, licenses, professional affiliations     |
| **Experiential expertise** | Non-YMYL topics (hobbies, consumer products, how-to) | Deep knowledge demonstrated through consistent, accurate content |

**Kyle Roof:** _"Google is not in the valued judgment game — they don't want to decide whether a Harvard degree beats 25 years of field experience with no degrees. What both come down to is: is a real person writing this?"_

**Per Google's QRG:** For YMYL topics, content should be written or reviewed by experts who demonstrably know the topic well. For non-YMYL topics, everyday expertise (life experience, personal use) is acceptable and often valuable.

**Content-type priority for expertise:**

| Content Type          | E-E-A-T Priority                                                         | Source       |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------ |
| **Medical/health**    | Expertise is top priority — must show credentials                        | Matthew Zare |
| **Finance/legal**     | Expertise is non-negotiable — credentials required                       | Matthew Zare |
| **Research blogs**    | Experience is the queen — show you're a real person who did the research | David Gunner |
| **Consumer products** | Experience is primary — have you actually used it?                       | Semrush      |
| **How-to/technical**  | Both expertise and experience matter                                     | Google's QRG |

---

### Authoritativeness (A)

Authoritativeness reflects **recognition by others in the field** — peers, industry, and the broader web acknowledge you as a go-to source.

**Three levels Google assesses (Semrush):**

| Level             | What It Measures                                                                |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Author level**  | Personal reputation, credentials, industry recognition of the individual author |
| **Content level** | Whether this specific piece is authoritative                                    |
| **Site level**    | Whether the overall site is recognized as an authority in its niche             |

**Gary Illyes confirmed:** _"E-A-T is largely based on links and mentions on authoritative sites. If the Washington Post mentions you, that's good."_

**Authoritativeness signals:**

| Signal                                   | Effect                                |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Backlinks from authoritative niche sites | Primary measurable signal             |
| Media mentions and press coverage        | Builds entity recognition             |
| Industry awards and recognition          | Signals peer validation               |
| Citations by other experts               | "Cited by" as a trust signal          |
| Guest posts on authoritative sites       | Builds both author and site authority |

**Per Ahrefs:** Authority is always relative — Tesla is an authority on electric vehicles but not on SEO. Topical authoritativeness is topic-specific.

---

### Trustworthiness (T) — The Most Important Component

> _"Trust is the most important member of the E-E-A-T family because untrustworthy pages have low E-E-A-T no matter how Experienced, Expert, or Authoritative they may seem."_ — **Google Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines**

**Google's own example:** _"A financial scam is untrustworthy, even if the content creator is a highly experienced and expert scammer who is considered the go-to on running scams!"_

**Trustworthiness signals:**

| Category         | Signals                                                      |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Technical**    | SSL/HTTPS, functional contact forms                          |
| **Content**      | Accurate information, error-free writing, proper citations   |
| **Transparency** | Named authors with bios, clear about page purpose            |
| **Business**     | Physical address, phone number, clear About page             |
| **Disclosure**   | Affiliate/sponsored content clearly marked, AI use disclosed |
| **Design**       | No excessive ads, no dark patterns, clean UX                 |
| **Reviews**      | Responding to reviews (positive AND negative)                |

**Kyle Roof's practical signals checklist:**

- Physical address on page
- Multiple contact methods (not just a form)
- Privacy policy, terms of service
- Refund/cancellation policies
- Current copyright date
- Organization schema
- Author bio pages with credentials
- Team pages

_"Real companies don't have issues putting their address on the page."_

## 7.3 Author Schema Markup and Author Entities

**Google's official position (Search Central, Dec 2025):**

> _"We strongly encourage adding accurate authorship information, such as bylines to content where readers might expect it."_

> _"Is it self-evident who authored the content? Do bylines lead to further information about the author?"_

**Author schema markup types:**

```html
<!-- Person Schema on author bio page -->
<script type="application/ld+json">
  {
    "@context": "https://schema.org",
    "@type": "Person",
    "name": "Author Name",
    "url": "https://example.com/author/author-name",
    "sameAs": [
      "https://linkedin.com/in/authorprofile",
      "https://twitter.com/authorhandle"
    ],
    "jobTitle": "Senior Analyst",
    "worksFor": { "@type": "Organization", "name": "Site Name" }
  }
</script>
```

**Does Author Schema directly improve rankings?**

No — but it helps Google attribute content to verified entities, which feeds into E-E-A-T evaluation. Per Scott Redgate: _"Schema markup does not magically help you rank higher, but it can help you take advantage of special features in search results because Google can now accurately see everything on your page."_

**What matters more than schema:**

| Factor                                | Why It Matters                                      |
| ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Consistent bylines                    | Builds author entity recognition over time          |
| Linked to bio pages                   | Humans and search engines can trace authorship      |
| Social profiles (especially LinkedIn) | Establishes the person as a real, verifiable entity |
| Organization schema                   | Establishes site-level entity                       |

## 7.5 Topical Backlinks vs. Generic Backlinks

**Direct answer:** Topical relevance provides a **multiplier** to link value, but raw domain authority often overrides topical mismatch.

**Key mechanisms confirming this:**

| Mechanism                    | Source                     | What It Means                                                                                                                                                                           |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Topic-Sensitive PageRank** | Moz (citing Google patent) | Pages ranked by relevance to specific topics — a link from a sports page to another sports page carries more topic-weighted PageRank                                                    |
| **Reasonable Surfer**        | Moz (citing Google patent) | _"When a topical cluster associated with the source document is related to a topical cluster associated with the target document, the link has a higher probability of being selected"_ |
| **Phrase-based Indexing**    | Moz                        | Google scores link relevancy by related phrases in source and target pages — low-relevance links get discounted                                                                         |

**Practical link value matrix:**

| Link Type                             | Value              | Example                                                      |
| ------------------------------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Topically relevant + high authority   | **Highest**        | Nutrition blog linking to your recipe site                   |
| Topically irrelevant + high authority | **Still valuable** | NASA linking to your SEO blog (authority outweighs mismatch) |
| Topically irrelevant + low authority  | **Low value**      | Random blog linking to your site                             |
| Pattern of mostly irrelevant links    | **Warning sign**   | Google may scrutinize for manipulation                       |

**Paddy Moogan (Moz):** _"Link relevance matters a lot less than content relevance. Anyone can link to you for any reason — but you control your own content. Google can hold you accountable for content relevance in a way they can't for incoming links."_

## 7.6 Unlinked Brand Mentions and Topical Authority

**Value of unlinked mentions:**

| Mechanism            | Effect                                                                      |
| -------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Co-occurrence        | Google notes which brands are mentioned together in topic-relevant contexts |
| Entity recognition   | Repeated mentions reinforce the association between a brand and its topic   |
| AI/LLM discovery     | AI systems use mentions as a discovery signal even without links            |
| Conversion potential | Monitored mentions can be converted to links through outreach               |

**Search Engine Land:** _"Citations and mentions by LLMs are determined by the content you post and the extents to which people talk about your brand — no link volumes or domain authority scores mentioned here."_

**Priority framework for unlinked mentions (Search Engine Land):**

1. Authority and relevance of the site (domain authority)
2. Placement and context (editorial content > sidebar)
3. Conversion/traffic potential
4. Content freshness
5. Author relationship potential

## 7.7 Industry Citation Strategies

Getting recognized experts to cite, reference, or link to your content is among the most powerful authoritativeness signals.

| Tactic                      | Method                                                         |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Original research**       | Publish unique data, surveys, or studies others will cite      |
| **Expert interviews**       | Quote recognized experts — they may link when shared           |
| **Resource page inclusion** | Get listed on industry resource pages                          |
| **Guest posting**           | Write for recognized publications with bio link                |
| **Broken link building**    | Find broken links on authoritative sites; suggest your content |
| **Digital PR**              | Create newsworthy content that earns organic citations         |
